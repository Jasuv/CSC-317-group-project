What does 1.5°C global warming mean?

1.5°C of global warming refers to a world where global average combined land surface air and sea surface temperatures are 1.5°C warmer than the average of the 51-year period 1850-1900. This is equivalent to being 0.87°C warmer than the 20-year period 1986-2005 or 0.63°C warmer than the decade 2006-2015.

How close are we to reaching 1.5°C warming?

Human activities have already caused approximately 1°C of global warming above pre-industrial levels as of 2017. If the current warming rate continues, the world is projected to reach 1.5°C of warming around 2040. However, the exact year that 1.5°C will be reached is uncertain and is projected to be between 2030 and 2052. This range is influenced by factors such as the chosen confidence level and the timeframe used to estimate the current rate of warming.

What are the potential impacts of reaching 1.5°C warming?

Even with a 1.5°C increase in global average temperature, warming will be greater than 1.5°C in many regions and seasons. At this level of warming, the world can expect an increase in the frequency and/or intensity of extreme events like droughts, floods, and heat waves. Impacts will also include rising sea levels, changes in rainfall patterns, and increasing ocean acidification. These changes pose risks to various natural and human systems, including ecosystems, agriculture, infrastructure, human health, and socioeconomic systems.

How is warming distributed across different regions?

Global warming is not uniform and varies across different regions. Many regions of the world have already experienced greater regional-scale warming than the global average. For example, 20-40% of the global population has already experienced over 1.5°C of warming in at least one season. Impacts at 1.5°C of global warming depend on the pathway taken to 1.5°C. For example, impacts from a pathway that stays below 1.5°C throughout the 21st century will be different from a pathway that exceeds and then returns to 1.5°C. Additionally, the impacts of 1.5°C global warming will vary in both space and time.

Who is most vulnerable to the impacts of 1.5°C warming?

The poor and vulnerable are disproportionately affected by warming up to and beyond 1.5°C. Many regions with large populations, especially in low- and moderate-income countries, face significant poverty and unequal access to resources. This makes them more vulnerable to climate change impacts, particularly in small and medium-sized cities prone to hazards. Small islands, megacities, coastal regions, and high mountain ranges are also among the most affected. The report highlights that ethical considerations and equity are central to addressing the impacts of climate change.

Why is 1.5°C warming a critical threshold?

Many climate change risks increase non-linearly with increasing global mean surface temperature. This means that every increment of warming matters, and limiting warming to 1.5°C would result in significantly lower risks compared to higher levels of warming. The report also stresses the need to consider equity and ethical concerns when evaluating different warming levels. This is because the impacts of warming are not distributed equally and will disproportionately affect the poor and vulnerable. 1.5°C.

What happens if global warming temporarily exceeds 1.5°C (an "overshoot")?

If global warming temporarily exceeds 1.5°C before returning to that level by or before 2100, it is considered an "overshoot" pathway. Even if temperatures return to 1.5°C, an overshoot would lead to more severe impacts than staying below 1.5°C. Some impacts, such as sea level rise, would continue to increase even after global mean surface temperature stabilizes. This means that an overshoot would lead to a higher sea level than scenarios where warming remains below 1.5°C.
