What does 1.5°C global warming mean?

1.5°C of global warming is when the average land, air, and sea surface temperatures collectively rise by 1.5°C above the average recorded from 1850 to 1900. The risks with climate change escalate non-linearly, meaning that every increment matters. 1.5°C of warming is the upper limit, and anything further would cause significant change.

What are the potential impacts of reaching 1.5°C of warming?

At 1.5°C, we can expect more frequent and/or intense weather events, including droughts, floods, and heatwaves. Other impacts include: rising sea levels, altered rainfall patterns, and heightened ocean acidification, which all pose risks to our ecosystems, agriculture, infrastructure, human health, and socioeconomic systems.

How close are we to reaching 1.5°C of warming?

As of 2017, human activities have already contributed to about 1°C of warming above pre-industrial levels. If current trends persist, the world is projected to reach 1.5°C around 2040, although this date may vary due to human-environment interactions.

Who is most vulnerable to the impacts of 1.5°C of warming?

The most vulnerable populations are the low-income or isolated countries, who are disproportionately affected due to limited access to resources. Small islands, megacities, coastal regions, and high mountain areas are particularly at more risk.

What happens if global warming temporarily exceeds 1.5°C ("overshoot")?

If global temperatures exceed 1.5°C before stabilizing, that is called an "overshoot" pathway. And if we ever find ourselves on that path, the impacts would be permanent, regardless if they were to drop below 1.5°C again. More glaciers would melt and our world topography would change significantly.

What can we do to prevent 1.5°C of global warming?

Sadly 1.5°C of global warming is inevitable, but there are many proposed ideas to limit warming to 1.5°C. Reducing Co2 emissions and finding clean and sustainable green energy. Scientists suggest a "carbon budget" where emissions are kept within a budget to reduce annual Co2 emissions. This budget should gradually be reduced until we reach a net zero of Co2 emission.
